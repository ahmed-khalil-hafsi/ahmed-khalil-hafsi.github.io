Introduction

Effective negotiation requires self-awareness about your natural tendencies and the ability to adapt. This assessment helps you understand your default style, strengths, weaknesses, and develop strategies for flexibility.

Gain insights into how you typically approach negotiations, how others perceive you, and how to leverage your style while developing complementary skills.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify your primary and secondary negotiation styles
  • Understand strengths and limitations of different approaches
  • Recognize how your style affects interactions
  • Develop strategies to adapt your style
  • Create a personal development plan

Understanding Negotiation Styles

Negotiation styles are patterns in how individuals approach conflict and interaction. Your style is influenced by personality, experience, culture, and context.

While most have a dominant style, effective negotiators adapt. This assessment identifies your tendencies and helps build flexibility.

The Five Negotiation Styles

Competing

Approach: Assertive, uncooperative

Use When: Crisis, unpopular actions
Drawbacks: Damages relationships, resistance

Accommodating

Approach: Unassertive, cooperative

Use When: Issue more important to other, preserve harmony
Drawbacks: Sacrifice interests, exploitation

Avoiding

Approach: Unassertive, uncooperative

Use When: Trivial issues, high emotions, need info
Drawbacks: Issues unresolved, frustration

Collaborating

Approach: Assertive, cooperative

Use When: Concerns too important to compromise, creative solutions possible
Drawbacks: Time-consuming, inappropriate for simple issues

Compromising

Approach: Moderate assertiveness/cooperation

Use When: Moderately important goals, equal power, limited time
Drawbacks: Suboptimal solutions, focus on positions

Assessment Instructions

This assessment consists of 30 statements. For each, indicate how accurately it describes your typical approach using the scale provided.

For accurate results:

  • Answer based on actual behavior, not ideal behavior
  • Consider general tendencies
  • Complete in one sitting (10-15 mins)
  • Be honest—no right/wrong styles

Negotiating with Different Styles

Understanding style interactions helps you adapt:

Meeting a Competing Style

Characteristics: Direct, assertive, focus on own goals

Strategies: Be direct, use objective criteria, don't yield to pressure, focus on problem-solving

Meeting an Accommodating Style

Characteristics: Focus on relationship, yields easily

Strategies: Build rapport, appreciate flexibility, help them articulate interests, don't exploit

Meeting an Avoiding Style

Characteristics: Reluctant to engage, postpones

Strategies: Create safe environment, use indirect methods initially, break down issues, be patient

Meeting a Collaborating Style

Characteristics: Seeks understanding, creative, shares info

Strategies: Engage thoroughly, share interests, bring ideas, allow time

Meeting a Compromising Style

Characteristics: Seeks middle ground, pragmatic

Strategies: Create value before compromising, use objective criteria, have fallbacks

Becoming Style-Flexible

Adapt based on context: high stakes (collaborate), time pressure (compromise), power imbalance (prepare more), culture (research!), high emotion (avoid temporarily).
