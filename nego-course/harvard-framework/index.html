Harvard's 7 Element Framework

Master the powerful framework used by negotiation experts worldwide for effective preparation and execution

Introduction

The 7 Element Framework, developed by the Harvard Negotiation Project, provides a comprehensive approach to negotiation preparation and execution. This systematic framework helps negotiators analyze, prepare for, and conduct more effective negotiations by focusing on seven key elements.

In this module, you'll learn how to apply each element to your negotiations, from identifying underlying interests to crafting clear commitments. You'll develop practical skills for using this framework to prepare for negotiations and navigate them successfully.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand and apply the 7 Element Framework to negotiation preparation
  • Distinguish between positions and interests in negotiation contexts
  • Develop and evaluate alternatives (BATNA) to strengthen your negotiation position
  • Generate creative options that satisfy multiple parties' interests
  • Identify and apply objective criteria to support your proposals
  • Improve communication effectiveness during negotiations
  • Manage relationships while addressing substantive issues
  • Create clear, realistic, and operational commitments

Overview of the 7 Element Framework

The 7 Element Framework provides a structured approach to negotiation that helps you prepare thoroughly and navigate complex discussions effectively. Each element addresses a critical aspect of negotiation:

1. Interests

The underlying needs, desires, concerns, and fears that motivate negotiators

2. Alternatives (BATNA)

What parties can do if they fail to reach an agreement

3. Relationships

The connection between negotiating parties and how it affects the process

4. Options

Possible agreements or parts of agreements that could satisfy both parties' interests

5. Legitimacy

Objective standards, criteria, or benchmarks that justify the fairness of proposals

6. Communication

The exchange of information, ideas, and perspectives throughout the negotiation

7. Commitments

Agreements, promises, and obligations that parties make to each other

By addressing each element systematically, you can approach negotiations with greater confidence, flexibility, and effectiveness.

The 7 Elements in Detail

1. Interests

Interests are the underlying needs, desires, concerns, and fears that motivate negotiators. They differ from positions, which are the stated demands or solutions that parties bring to the table.

Positions vs. Interests

Positions Interests
What someone says they want Why they want it
Specific solutions or demands Underlying needs and motivations
Often inflexible Can be satisfied in multiple ways
Visible and explicit Often hidden or unstated

Key Points About Interests:

  • Multiple interests are usually at play in any negotiation
  • Interests can include substantive (economic/business), procedural (how things are done), and relationship concerns
  • Understanding both your own interests and those of your counterpart is crucial
  • Asking "why" questions helps uncover hidden interests

Example:

Position: "I want three days off next month."

Possible Interests:

  • Attending a planned family vacation
  • Scheduling medical appointments
  • Recovering from burnout
  • Attending a professional development event

Understanding these underlying interests opens up more solution possibilities than simply focusing on the three-day position.

Application Tips:

  • Prepare by listing your own interests in priority order
  • Anticipate your counterpart's interests through research and empathy
  • Use open-ended questions to uncover interests during negotiation
  • Look for shared or compatible interests to build upon
  • Acknowledge the legitimacy of others' interests even when you disagree

2. Alternatives (BATNA)

Alternatives represent what parties can do if they fail to reach an agreement. The Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement (BATNA) is the most favorable option available if negotiations fail.

Key Points About Alternatives:

  • Your BATNA determines your negotiation power
  • Improving your BATNA strengthens your position
  • Understanding the other party's BATNA helps gauge their willingness to agree
  • Never accept a deal worse than your BATNA
  • WATNA (Worst Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement) sets your bottom line

Example:

When negotiating to buy a car, your BATNA might include:

  • A firm offer from another dealer for a similar vehicle at $28,000
  • The option to purchase a different model that meets most of your needs for $26,000
  • The possibility of waiting three months for new models to be released, potentially lowering current prices

Knowing these alternatives gives you a clear walkaway point and confidence in your negotiation.

Application Tips:

  • Identify all possible alternatives before negotiation begins
  • Work to improve your BATNA before and during negotiations
  • Evaluate your counterpart's BATNA realistically
  • Be careful about revealing your BATNA—it can become a ceiling for what you might achieve
  • Use your BATNA to determine when to walk away from a deal

3. Relationships

The relationship between negotiating parties affects both the process and outcomes of negotiations. It can determine how fixed your stance is on certain positions and what negotiation approach you take.

Key Points About Relationships:

  • Consider the importance of the relationship (one-time deal vs. ongoing partnership)
  • Separate relationship issues from substantive issues
  • Build rapport and trust before and during negotiations
  • Consider reputation effects even in one-time negotiations
  • Plan how to handle relationship challenges that may arise

Example:

When negotiating with a long-term supplier:

If you push too hard on price in a single negotiation, you might win on the immediate issue but damage the relationship, leading to less flexibility, reduced service quality, or reluctance to help in emergencies in the future.

A balanced approach that acknowledges the value of the ongoing relationship while still addressing your substantive needs is often more effective.

Application Tips:

  • Invest time in relationship building, especially in cultures where relationships are prioritized
  • Address relationship tensions directly but separately from substantive issues
  • Build trust through consistency, transparency, and keeping commitments
  • Consider the long-term impact of your negotiation approach on the relationship
  • Recognize that strong relationships can create more value over time

4. Options

Options are the possible agreements or parts of agreements that could satisfy both parties' interests. They represent the range of possibilities that negotiators might agree on.

Key Points About Options:

  • Options differ from alternatives (which are what happens if you don't reach agreement)
  • Brainstorm options before evaluating them
  • Create value through trades that capitalize on differences in interests, priorities, and valuations
  • Look for "win-win" solutions that benefit both parties
  • Avoid premature judgment of options

Example:

When buying a car, options might include:

  • Different financing arrangements (cash payment, dealer financing, third-party loan)
  • Various warranty packages (standard, extended, premium)
  • Included accessories or services (free maintenance, upgraded features)
  • Delivery timing (immediate, scheduled, custom order)

These options can be combined in various ways to create a satisfactory deal for both parties.

Application Tips:

  • Separate option generation from evaluation—brainstorm first, judge later
  • Look for differences in priorities that enable mutually beneficial trades
  • Consider packaging multiple issues together rather than resolving them one by one
  • Ask "what if" questions to explore creative possibilities
  • Build on the other party's ideas rather than dismissing them

5. Legitimacy

Legitimacy refers to objective standards, criteria, or benchmarks that can justify the fairness of a proposed agreement.

Key Points About Legitimacy:

  • External standards provide objective validation for proposals
  • Can include market value, precedent, expert opinion, law, or industry norms
  • Helps move beyond "your word against mine" arguments
  • Strengthens your position when standards support your offers
  • Can weaken counterpart's position when their offers lack legitimacy

Example:

When negotiating salary, legitimate standards might include:

  • Industry salary surveys showing median compensation for similar roles
  • The company's own published pay scales
  • Compensation packages for comparable positions within the organization
  • Cost-of-living data for the region

Referring to these standards makes your request appear fair and reasonable rather than arbitrary.

Application Tips:

  • Research relevant standards before negotiation begins
  • Present standards as objective rather than self-serving
  • Be prepared to question the relevance or application of standards presented by the other party
  • Agree on which standards to use before applying them
  • Use multiple standards to strengthen your position

6. Communication

Effective communication is essential throughout the negotiation process, from preparation to implementation.

Key Points About Communication:

  • Active listening is as important as clear speaking
  • Ask open-ended questions to gather information
  • Acknowledge what's been said to demonstrate understanding
  • Clarify and probe to uncover interests
  • Be mindful of non-verbal signals
  • Speak about interests rather than just positions

Example:

In a client negotiation:

  • Instead of saying "Your deadline is impossible," you might ask "What factors are driving your timeline?"
  • Rather than stating "We can't do it for that price," you could explain "Our costs for materials and labor require a minimum of $X to maintain quality standards."
  • When facing resistance, paraphrasing their concerns shows you're listening: "So your main concern is ensuring the project stays within budget, is that right?"

These communication approaches help maintain a productive dialogue while addressing substantive issues.

Application Tips:

  • Practice active listening techniques (paraphrasing, summarizing, questioning)
  • Balance advocacy (making your case) with inquiry (exploring their perspective)
  • Be aware of cultural differences in communication styles
  • Address misunderstandings immediately when they occur
  • Consider the timing and setting of difficult conversations

7. Commitments

Commitments are the agreements, promises, and obligations that parties make to each other during and at the conclusion of a negotiation.

Key Points About Commitments:

  • Well-crafted commitments are clear, realistic, and operational
  • Consider timing—when to make and when to seek commitments
  • Different levels of commitments exist (tentative, contingent, final)
  • Implementation details matter as much as the agreement itself
  • Include monitoring and review mechanisms for long-term agreements

Example:

In a business partnership agreement:

  • Weak commitment: "We'll provide regular updates on project progress."
  • Strong commitment: "We will deliver a detailed status report by the 15th of each month covering milestones achieved, budget status, and upcoming deliverables, with a review call scheduled within 3 business days of report delivery."

The strong commitment specifies what, when, and how, making it clear and enforceable.

Application Tips:

  • Be specific about who will do what, when, and how
  • Consider including consequences for non-compliance
  • Document commitments in writing when appropriate
  • Build in flexibility for changing circumstances when needed
  • Ensure all parties have the authority to make the commitments they offer
  • Create a process for resolving disagreements about implementation

Applying the Framework

The real power of the 7 Element Framework comes from using it as both a preparation tool and a diagnostic tool during negotiations.

Preparation Guidelines

  1. Create a preparation document with sections for each element
  2. Complete your analysis for your side first, then attempt to do the same from your counterpart's perspective
  3. Identify potential challenges and opportunities within each element
  4. Develop strategies to address weaknesses and leverage strengths
  5. Prioritize which elements need the most attention in your specific negotiation

Diagnostic Use During Negotiation

When negotiations become difficult, use the framework to diagnose the source of problems:

  • Are we failing to understand each other's interests?
  • Is one party's BATNA much stronger, creating a power imbalance?
  • Are relationship tensions interfering with substantive progress?
  • Have we generated enough options to find common ground?
  • Are we disagreeing about what standards are legitimate?
  • Is poor communication causing misunderstandings?
  • Are proposed commitments unclear or unrealistic?

Identifying which elements are causing difficulty allows you to address specific issues rather than getting stuck in positional arguments.

Conclusion

Harvard's 7 Element Framework provides a powerful structure for approaching negotiations systematically. By understanding and addressing interests, alternatives, relationships, options, legitimacy, communication, and commitments, you can navigate complex negotiations more effectively.

Remember that the elements are interconnected—progress in one area often creates momentum in others. With practice, this framework will become a natural part of your negotiation thinking, helping you achieve better outcomes while maintaining important relationships.
