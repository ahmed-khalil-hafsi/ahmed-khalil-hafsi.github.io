Introduction

The 7 Element Framework, developed by the Harvard Negotiation Project, provides a comprehensive approach to negotiation preparation and execution. This systematic framework helps negotiators analyze, prepare for, and conduct more effective negotiations by focusing on seven key elements.

In this module, you'll learn how to apply each element to your negotiations, from identifying underlying interests to crafting clear commitments. You'll develop practical skills for using this framework to prepare for negotiations and navigate them successfully.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand and apply the 7 Element Framework to negotiation preparation
  • Distinguish between positions and interests in negotiation contexts
  • Develop and evaluate alternatives (BATNA) to strengthen your negotiation position
  • Generate creative options that satisfy multiple parties' interests
  • Identify and apply objective criteria to support your proposals
  • Improve communication effectiveness during negotiations
  • Manage relationships while addressing substantive issues
  • Create clear, realistic, and operational commitments

Overview of the 7 Element Framework

The 7 Element Framework provides a structured approach to negotiation that helps you prepare thoroughly and navigate complex discussions effectively. Each element addresses a critical aspect of negotiation:

1. Interests

The underlying needs, desires, concerns, and fears

2. Alternatives (BATNA)

What parties can do if they fail to reach agreement

3. Relationships

The connection between parties and how it affects the process

4. Options

Possible agreements or parts of agreements

5. Legitimacy

Objective standards that justify fairness

6. Communication

The exchange of information and perspectives

7. Commitments

Agreements, promises, and obligations made

By addressing each element systematically, you can approach negotiations with greater confidence, flexibility, and effectiveness.

The 7 Elements in Detail

1. Interests

Interests are the underlying needs, desires, concerns, and fears... They differ from positions...

Positions vs. Interests

PositionsInterests
What someone says they wantWhy they want it
Specific solutionsUnderlying motivations

Key Points:

  • Multiple interests usually exist
  • Can include substantive, procedural, relationship
  • Understanding both sides is crucial

Example:

Position: "I want three days off."

Possible Interests: Family vacation, medical needs, burnout.

Application Tips:

  • Prepare by listing interests
  • Anticipate counterpart's interests
  • Use open-ended questions

2. Alternatives (BATNA)

Alternatives represent what parties can do if they fail to reach an agreement. The Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement (BATNA)...

Key Points:

  • BATNA determines power
  • Improving BATNA strengthens position
  • Never accept less than BATNA

Example:

Buying a car BATNA: Offer from another dealer, different model, wait for new releases.

3. Relationships

The relationship between negotiating parties affects both the process and outcomes...

Key Points:

  • Consider relationship importance (one-time vs. ongoing)
  • Separate relationship from substance
  • Build rapport and trust

Example:

Negotiating with long-term supplier: Pushing too hard damages relationship, affecting future service/flexibility.

4. Options

Options are the possible agreements or parts of agreements that could satisfy interests...

Key Points:

  • Options differ from alternatives
  • Brainstorm before evaluating
  • Create value through trades

Example:

Car purchase options: Financing, warranties, accessories, delivery timing.

5. Legitimacy

Legitimacy refers to objective standards, criteria, or benchmarks that justify fairness...

Key Points:

  • External standards provide objective validation
  • Includes market value, precedent, law, norms
  • Strengthens position

Example:

Salary negotiation standards: Industry surveys, company pay scales, cost-of-living data.

6. Communication

Effective communication is essential throughout the negotiation process...

Key Points:

  • Active listening is key
  • Ask open-ended questions
  • Acknowledge understanding

Example:

Instead of "Deadline impossible," ask "What factors drive timeline?" Use paraphrasing: "So your main concern is..."

7. Commitments

Commitments are the agreements, promises, and obligations parties make...

Key Points:

  • Well-crafted commitments are clear, realistic, operational
  • Consider timing
  • Include monitoring

Example:

Strong commitment: "Deliver status report by 15th monthly..." vs. Weak: "Provide regular updates."

Applying the Framework

The real power of the 7 Element Framework comes from using it as both a preparation tool and a diagnostic tool during negotiations.

Preparation Guidelines

  1. Create a preparation document for each element
  2. Analyze your side, then counterpart's perspective
  3. Identify challenges and opportunities
  4. Develop strategies
  5. Prioritize elements

Diagnostic Use During Negotiation

When negotiations become difficult, diagnose the source:

  • Failing to understand interests?
  • BATNA imbalance?
  • Relationship tensions interfering?
  • Not enough options generated?
  • Disagreeing on legitimacy?
  • Poor communication?
  • Unclear commitments?

Conclusion

Harvard's 7 Element Framework provides a powerful structure for approaching negotiations systematically. By understanding and addressing interests, alternatives, relationships, options, legitimacy, communication, and commitments, you can navigate complex negotiations more effectively.

Remember that the elements are interconnected—progress in one area often creates momentum in others. With practice, this framework will become a natural part of your negotiation thinking, helping you achieve better outcomes while maintaining important relationships.
