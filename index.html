How I think about negotiation

Negotiation is structural analysis. Every negotiation sits inside a system of interests, constraints, and expectations, and the outcome is largely set before anyone speaks: by who holds leverage, what each side genuinely needs, and how their incentives line up under pressure and incomplete information.

Get that structure right and the tactics follow. So my work starts upstream of the table. I map the ecosystem, locate the real leverage, and design the architecture of the agreement: how pricing, indexation, and terms allocate value and risk across the whole life of the relationship.

Structure first. Diagnose the system before choosing words.

Leverage is built. Alternatives, information, and timing can be engineered.

Design for the whole horizon. The terms govern for years; price is day one.

Read the incentives. People are predictable once you see what they optimize for.

Game theory, in practice

Game theory is a practical lens. It takes a messy, emotional situation and asks the disciplined questions. Who are the real players, including the ones not in the room? What are their payoffs? Which moves are credible? Where can a credible commitment, a visible exit option, or careful signaling shift the equilibrium in your favor?

Add behavioral science, how people actually decide under risk, framing, and time pressure, and negotiation becomes legible and closer to engineering. You shape the game itself.

What I'm exploring now

Technology is rewriting the table. I am especially interested in AI and large language models, both as tools that change how we prepare and as negotiating actors in their own right. What does it mean to bargain against a counterparty that runs on probability? When should an autonomous agent stay silent? I write about these questions as they develop, alongside the enduring ones: supplier networks, leverage in concentrated markets, and the strategic logic behind geopolitical power shifts.
